Use Cases

Where AI agents move money,
Kontext builds the audit trail.

Three scenarios. Each shows the compliance exposure when agents drive transactions autonomously — and what changes when every payment is logged, screened, and exportable.

Use Case 01

Cross-border money movement
for invoicing

The situation

AI agents process supplier invoices and initiate USDC transfers across jurisdictions — US to Mexico, EU to Philippines, UK to Nigeria. Agents match invoices to purchase orders, validate amounts, and execute payment autonomously. Hundreds of transfers per week. No human reviews individual payments.

The compliance exposure

Travel Rule triggers at $3,000. There is no proof the agent verified the counterparty before the transfer. No record ties the invoice to the payment. If an examiner asks "why did your system send $28,000 to a wallet in Lagos?" — the answer is buried across four systems and three time zones. Reconstructing the decision path takes days.

What Kontext does

Each transfer runs through verify(). Kontext logs the transfer with agent ID, workflow context (invoice-payment), route (GB→NG), and screens both wallets against OFAC, OFSI, and OpenSanctions. The intent hash binds the invoice reference to the payment cryptographically. The evidence bundle is exportable in one click.

What changes

The compliance officer doesn't need to reconstruct anything. Every cross-border payment has a complete evidence trail: who sent it, what triggered it, which invoice it fulfills, whether both wallets were screened, and a digest chain link that proves the record hasn't been modified after the fact.

The examiner scenario
"Show me the authorization chain for this cross-border payment."
Click the payment in the dashboard. Evidence drawer shows: invoice ID in metadata, agent authorization scope, OFAC clear on both wallets, policy evaluation (GENIUS Act threshold flagged — agent had documented approval to proceed), digest chain link #47,291. Export as signed JSON. The examiner has everything in 10 seconds.
Use Case 02

Payroll transfers to a global
contractor workforce

The situation

A platform uses AI agents to calculate and disburse contractor payments across 12 countries. Agents handle FX conversion timing, split payments, and route optimization. Hundreds of transfers per pay cycle. Each contractor has a wallet address, not a bank account.

The compliance exposure

CTR threshold at $10,000 for aggregate payments. There is no way to prove each contractor wallet was screened before payment. No audit trail connects the payroll calculation to the actual disbursement. If a contractor wallet appears on a sanctions list after the fact — can you prove it was clean at the time of payment?

What Kontext does

Each disbursement goes through verify() — screening the contractor wallet, logging the payroll-run ID, flagging CTR thresholds. The digest chain proves the screening result was recorded before the transfer, not retroactively. Batch audit export covers the entire pay cycle in a single file.

What changes

The compliance officer can answer "were all contractor wallets screened before the March 15 payroll run?" in seconds, not days. Every transfer has a timestamp-proven screening result. The digest chain makes retroactive record insertion mathematically detectable.

The examiner scenario
"Were all contractor wallets screened before the March 15 payroll run?"
Export the audit trail filtered by workflow=payroll-disbursement and date range. Every transfer shows screening timestamp, result, and provider. Digest chain proves no records were inserted or modified after the fact. The export is self-verifying — any third party can validate it independently without Kontext.
Use Case 03

Risk control for
vendor payments

The situation

AI agents manage vendor relationships — approving purchase orders, releasing milestone payments, handling early-payment discounts. Agents operate within policy limits but make autonomous decisions about timing and routing. The finance team sees the results, not the decisions.

The compliance exposure

An agent pays a vendor $14,500 that exceeds the org's per-transaction limit. Another agent routes through an unfamiliar chain. A third pays a wallet that was added to the SDN list 6 hours ago. Without a compliance layer, the risk team finds out from the examiner, not from the system.

What Kontext does

Policy evaluation runs on every payment — payout limits, allowed chains, blocked countries. Violations are logged with the specific rule that fired. Trust scoring drops when agents hit policy boundaries. Anomaly detection flags unusual patterns: new destination, off-hours activity, rapid succession. The compliance officer sees warnings before the examiner does.

What changes

The risk team moves from reactive to proactive. Policy violations are documented in real-time with the exact rule, threshold, and agent scope. When an examiner asks about a violation, the answer includes what happened, what control fired, and what action was taken — all cryptographically linked in the digest chain.

The examiner scenario
"Your agent exceeded the payout limit on March 12. What controls were in place?"
Dashboard shows the policy violation was logged at 17:48. The payment was blocked (not just warned). The agent's trust score dropped from 88 to 76. The compliance officer was notified via the dashboard alert. Full evidence bundle: policy rule ID, threshold ($10,000), agent authorization scope, and digest chain proof that the block was enforced before the transfer could execute.
Use Case 04

AI agent
payment networks

The situation

Agent-to-agent payment networks like Skyfire and Payman let AI agents autonomously send USDC to other agents and services. An LLM decides to procure data, pay for an API call, or settle an invoice — all without a human clicking "approve." The agents move fast because that's the point.

The compliance exposure

Nobody authorized this specific payment. Someone authorized the agent to make payments like this. When an examiner asks "who approved the $8,200 transfer at 3:14 AM?", the honest answer is "a policy file written by a developer six months ago, interpreted by a model." That's a fundamentally different compliance posture — and most companies haven't thought through what it means for their BSA program.

What Kontext does

Every agent-initiated payment gets an authorization binding: which policy authorized it, what the agent's reasoning was, and the trust score at the moment of execution. The digest chain captures agent decision context that would otherwise be lost when the model's weights update. A2A attestation lets receiving agents verify the sender's compliance status before accepting payment.

What changes

The company can answer "who authorized this?" with specifics: policy ID, authorization scope, agent reasoning captured at transaction time, and a tamper-evident record that proves the compliance check ran before the transfer executed. The first enforcement action in agentic payments will set the precedent — companies using Kontext will have the evidence trail that others won't.

The examiner scenario
"Your AI agent sent $8,200 USDC to an unknown wallet at 3:14 AM. Who authorized this transaction?"
Dashboard shows the agent operated under policy treasury-ops-v3 with a $10,000 per-transaction limit. OFAC screening cleared at 3:14:02 AM. Agent reasoning log: "Invoice #4471 from verified vendor, amount within policy scope, destination wallet matched vendor registry." Trust score: 91/100. Full authorization chain — from policy creation to agent deployment to this specific execution — captured in digest chain link #12,847.
Use Case 05

White-label stablecoin
infrastructure

The situation

Platforms like SoFi and BVNK provide stablecoin infrastructure to downstream partners — banks, fintechs, and enterprise clients issue white-label stablecoins or integrate USDC into their settlement flows. The platform holds the license and carries the BSA obligations. The partners initiate the transactions and own the customer relationships.

The compliance exposure

Your partner gets the customer relationship and the revenue share. You get the examiner's letter. When a partner's end user sends $50,000 USDC to a sanctioned wallet through your infrastructure, you can't reverse it — stablecoin rails don't have ACH returns. The GENIUS Act requires all permitted stablecoin issuers to annually certify BSA compliance. That certification is yours, not your partner's.

What Kontext does

Multi-tenant compliance monitoring with per-partner audit isolation. Aggregate screening detects structuring across tenants — a user sending $3,200 through Partner A and $3,100 through Partner B within 4 hours triggers a unified alert, not two unremarkable line items. Per-partner compliance exports let each partner's regulator independently verify their activity.

What changes

The platform produces independently verifiable compliance artifacts — records your partner can validate without your involvement. When your partner's regulator asks for compliance evidence, you produce a signed export that includes the screening results, policy evaluations, and digest chain proofs for every transaction on their tenant. "Trust us, we're compliant" becomes "here's the cryptographic proof."

The examiner scenario
"A user split $9,600 across three of your partners in 4 hours. Did your monitoring detect this?"
Aggregate monitoring flagged the cross-tenant pattern at 14:22 UTC. Dashboard shows: Partner A ($3,200 at 12:08), Partner B ($3,100 at 13:41), Partner C ($3,300 at 15:52) — total $9,600, below CTR but consistent with structuring. Alert generated, SAR workflow initiated. Per-partner exports show each transaction with the aggregate flag attached. Digest chain proves the alert was generated in real-time, not retroactively.
Use Case 06

CPN cross-border
settlement

The situation

Circle's Comprehensive Payments Network connects stablecoin rails to traditional payment corridors — enabling USDC settlement for cross-border transfers that previously relied on correspondent banking. Partners like Thunes and Flutterwave use CPN to reach emerging markets where traditional rails are slow, expensive, or unavailable.

The compliance exposure

Cross-border stablecoin settlement triggers Travel Rule, OFAC, and multi-jurisdiction BSA obligations simultaneously. A $15,000 USDC transfer from the US to Nigeria crosses the Travel Rule threshold ($3K), requires OFAC screening on both endpoints, and creates record-keeping obligations in at least two jurisdictions. CPN handles the settlement. Nobody handles the compliance evidence.

What Kontext does

Every CPN settlement gets a compliance envelope: dual-endpoint OFAC screening, Travel Rule data capture, amount threshold evaluation, and jurisdiction-specific checks. The audit trail links the originating fiat instruction to the on-chain USDC transfer to the destination corridor — a single evidence chain across the entire settlement lifecycle.

What changes

The compliance team has a unified view across corridors, not siloed logs per payment rail. When regulators in different jurisdictions ask about the same transfer, both get the same cryptographically verified evidence bundle. The digest chain proves that screening ran before settlement, not after — which matters when the transaction is irreversible.

The examiner scenario
"Show me the compliance evidence for the $15,000 USDC transfer from your US entity to Flutterwave's Nigerian corridor on March 8."
Evidence bundle contains: originator KYC reference, OFAC screening (clear, both endpoints), Travel Rule data package (originator name, account, address + beneficiary details), amount threshold evaluation ($15,000 > $3K EDD, < $10K CTR), CPN corridor confirmation, and on-chain settlement hash. All linked in digest chain link #31,204. Screening timestamp precedes settlement timestamp by 1.2 seconds. Tamper-evident.
